"I hate to bother you if this isn't relevant…"

It sounds polite.
But underneath it is something else.

A subtle apology for existing.

When you lead with "sorry to bother you," you're quietly saying:
"I might not belong here."
"I might be an interruption."
"I might be wasting your time."

That tension is felt before the conversation even starts.

Your energy arrives before your words.

If you show up unsure, the other person has to manage that uncertainty.
They brace.
They protect.
They disengage.

But notice what your role is.

You're not knocking on doors selling encyclopedias.
You're not asking for a favor.
You're doing work that surfaces friction people might be living with.

That isn't a bother.
It's often a relief.

So instead of apologizing for the conversation, just enter it.

Calmly.
Detached.
Neutrally.
Curious.
Without an agenda.

"When calls come in after hours, are you using an answering service, automated text messages saying you'll follow up in the morning, or an AI dispatcher that takes the call and books the appointment?"

You're not an interruption.
You're an invitation.

Is happiness a skill you can learn?

I think it is.

First, what takes happiness away?

Conditional living.

If/then statements.

If I get the promotion, then I'll be happy.
If I make $200k a year, then I'll be happy.
If I buy the bigger house, then I'll be happy.

The problem?

The finish line keeps moving.

You get the thing.

Then the mind creates a new condition.

Comparison does the same thing.

You have a beautiful home.

Then you see someone with a bigger one.

Suddenly what was once enough feels smaller.

Now what creates happiness?

Absorption.

When you're fully immersed in something, happiness often shows up on its own.

The flow state.

You lose track of time.

Playing an instrument.
Painting.
Fishing.
Doing work you love.

Just being in it.

Another one:

Contentment.

Being okay with what you already have instead of constantly reaching for more.

Gratitude helps here.

Millions of people would consider their prayers answered to have your life.

And maybe the most practical skill:

Daily meditation.

Sitting quietly for 20 minutes.

Observing thoughts without becoming them.

Over time, this builds a tolerance for stillness.

And a tolerance for boredom.

So when the urge arises to scroll, buy, distract, or compare…

you can simply notice it.

What if nothing is missing except your attention to what's already here?

Chances are, you've heard this on a cold call:

"What do you want?!"

Said with a little edge.

Don't get flustered.
Don't slip into a pitch.
Don't Voss them with, "Sounds like you get a lot of these calls."

Lean back.
Pause.
Then answer the question calmly.

"I'm calling because I had a question about how you're scheduling jobs that come in after hours."

That's it.

Why it works:

They're asking for clarity.

"Why are you calling?"

The moment people feel like you're hiding the reason for the call, resistance goes up.

The moment you answer directly, tension drops.

Now they can decide whether it's worth continuing.

Sometimes the fastest way to lower resistance
is simply answering the question.

I've tried a lot of ways to open a cold call.

This is one that worked for me and thousands of sellers.

Doesn't mean it'll work for you.

Everything I share is an experiment.

Run it.
See what happens.
Find your own way.

Hello?
Josh?
Yes.
Hi, I'm Ben. We've never spoken, but I was hoping you could help me for a moment.
Sure.
Do you still handle scheduling for your plumbers when people call in?
I do.
Bit of a random question…

When calls come in after hours, are you using an answering service, automated text messages saying you'll follow up in the morning, or an AI dispatcher that takes the call and books the appointment?

The psychology:

"We've never spoken" lowers the guard.

"Could you help me for a moment?" gives them control.

"Do you still handle scheduling…" confirms you're talking to the right person using their language, not sales language.

Then the A / B / C question gets them thinking about how they currently solve the problem.

A - current way
B - current way
C - what's possible

No pitch.
No explanation.

Just a question that makes the status quo visible.

Don't start with what you sell.
Start with how they currently get the job done.
