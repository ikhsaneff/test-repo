My sales team has been working on improving our question-asking skills on discovery calls.
Here were my top takeaways from yesterday's training:

1. It's not about asking the perfect question; it's about driving the call in the right direction.

Your questions don't need to be perfect, but they should be genuine and focused on either:
 A) Asking about their problem
 B) Diagnosing the root cause of their problem so we can fix it

Focus your energy on asking about problems & their root causes. Worry less about phrasing things in the most perfect eloquent manner.

---

2. Synthesize and Steer

Any time the prospect shares a lot of info, run this play:
 A) Synthesize what they said in a quick recap,
 B) STEER the conversation in the direction of problems your product solves.

If you don't steer the convo, you'll burn valuable time meandering around information that isn't relevant to your sale.

You gotta drive the conversation to your "winning zone"

----

3. How have you tried to solve this so far?

If your prospect is talking to you (a vendor), then they're at their last resort.

Most people try to solve their problems on their own before talking to a seller.

You'll learn a TON about their efforts to date and why they NEED outside help when you ask them how they've tried to solve the problem thus far.

You'll usually hear what went well, but where they failed on their own. That's where your product fits in :)

In my team's weekly sales training on Monday, I did a pretty hard reset with my reps about how using AI was hurting our outbound efforts.
Our mistake + how we fixed it:

--

Like many sales teams, we use Claude to do account research + sift through signals to help us draft hyper-personalized outbound messages that feel VERY tailored.

Problem is, they all STANK of AI.

When I read some of the outbound emails my team was sending it felt like I was reading POETRY, not a business email sent by a human being.

This "uncanny valley" of writing was hurting our outbound meetings booking rate, despite our messages technically having MORE research + personalization included in them.

Prospects want to hear from human beings, not robots.

--

6 Changes We Made:

1. Use AI to draft V1 of the email, reps now re-write V2 like a human.

2. Simpler writing. Gorgeous prose doesn't matter if the prospect can't quickly grasp what you're saying.

3. We care less about perfect spelling/grammer/formatting. We're writing for comprehension, which sometimes includes using informal writing style.

4. More voice notes (short; 10-15 seconds).

5. When we send a voice note, we ALSO include a TL;DL (too long; didn't listen). Sending both is getting us more replies.

6. Including screenshots of the research we did. I learned this one from Darren McKee. Screenshot what you saw that made you reach out (press release; job description, something on their site) and send that to them when reaching out.

One of the bigger mistakes I've made as a sales leader has been teaching my reps too much about selling at once.
I love the psychology of selling, but it started to backfire before big calls:

Problem:
I've accumulated massive amounts of sales knowledge from running 30MPC for 6 years. I've literally interviewed 400+ top 1% performers about the techniques they use to win deals.

Problem:
Throwing 400+ interviews worth of sales tips at a rep an hour before their big demo actually makes things more confusing. It's like trying to teach a wrestler a brand new move 45 minutes before a big match.

Mark Kosoglow put me in my place on a recent episode of 30MPC:

He said: "Nick, if you're telling me you taught your reps 57 things this quarter, the truth is you actually taught them ZERO".

Whoops!

---

The change I've made to help my team prep before big calls:

FIRST: We align on the call structure. What are the "chapters" of the call we need to hit?

SECOND: I help them with the ONE big point they need to make per chapter of the call. No more than that.

THIRD: We roleplay the most important part of the call 2-3 times. That's it.

Our calls are going much better as a result.

Prospect hung up on you?
The next time you call 'em should actually be EASIER.
Try this technique:

___

You call Armand at 1:15 on a Tuesday. After your opener, he says:

"I can't talk now, I'm crazy busy preparing for a board meeting"

*click*

___

BEFORE you move to your next dial, make note of the hangup details:

- Call date
- Call time
- EXACT words he said before hanging up

Create a task to call again in a few days, but switch your opener to reference the last call:

"Armand, you and I last spoke on March 24th at 1:15 in the afternoon. At the time, you didn't have time because you were 'crazy busy preparing for a board meeting'.

Know I'm being persistent, but mind if I take a sec to share what I saw that made me call back and then YOU can tell me if it's worth a conversation?"

___

Parroting the exact words they used is powerful:

1. Signals that you're persistent, not just a spammer who blindly dials a list. You want to talk to them specifically and they are so important to me that you're willing to call back again.

2. The use of the exact words pre hangup gets them to sit up and say "Huh, I guess I did talk to this guy". Most cold callers blend in and are forgotten. If you can get remembered, your chance of success improves substantially.
