Here's my 4-step LinkedIn automation sequence:

Step 1: Blank connect (no message)

This gets way more accepts than pitching in the request.

Step 2: 1-2 sentence open-ended question

Something low-friction and relevant to their role. Goal here is to spark a reply, not pitch. Just take your email and turn it into a text that you would send a friend.

Step 3: Offer a helpful resource

Share something that actually helps. Could be a checklist, guide, short Loom.

Step 4: Short bump + CTA

A quick follow-up to keep the convo alive and see if they want to chat.

There are 9 variables I test in a cold email.

They're not equally important, and the order you test them in matters.

Here's the hierarchy, from most impactful to least (in my opinion... of course):

(1) The offer and CTA.

This is the biggest lever by a wide margin. Most "meeting ask" CTAs cap out at 2 to 3% reply rates. Offer-based CTAs, a real audit, industry benchmarks, a short video, something concrete, consistently outperform them.

(2) The hook.

Your first line decides whether they keep reading. It needs to make the reason for reaching out feel believable. Not flattery, not fake personalization, not a marketing-style setup. A real observation, a pointed question, or relevant social proof.

(3) The problem statement.

This should come directly from your sales call recordings, not your website. Your website is one-to-many. Sales calls are one-to-one. If your problem line sounds like something any competitor could write, it's not specific enough.

(4) The value prop.

Your value prop has two jobs: show you understand what they want, then explain how you deliver it in a way that's actually believable. If it sounds like a generic claim any competitor could make, rewrite it.

Those are the top 4.

The rest are: Social proof, objection handling, personalization, tone, and subject line.

These matter, but only after the first four are working.

Don't layer in personalization to save an email that hasn't converted on the fundamentals yet.

9 times out of 10 your reply rates max out at 1-2% when the CTA is purely a meeting ask.

If you want to get above that, you need to give something first.

A few examples from campaigns we've run:

(1) An email deliverability tool offered a free audit of the prospect's sending domains. The CTA was: "Happy to run a report on your SDR team's domains and inboxes to spot a few areas where you might be able to improve deliverability and increase reply rates. Would that be of interest?"

(2) A marketing agency offered a free AI-generated brand sentiment analysis the prospect could take back to their leadership team. The CTA was: "We can run an analysis on your brand sentiment and send back a comprehensive report for you to review with your team. It only takes 10 to 15 minutes."

(3) A community platform offered a benchmarking report showing how similar communities were driving engagement and retention.

Notice what all three have in common.

- they're specific
- they deliver something real
- the prospect can say yes without committing to anything

The meeting comes after.

Once they've received the thing you promised, you make it the natural next step: "Happy to walk you through the details live if you want to see how other teams solved these issues."

The offer builds trust. The meeting is just the continuation.
