Team selling boosts win rates by 50%. But most team selling looks like an 11pm uncoordinated salsa dance.
Instead, assign your counterpart 1 of 4 roles:

1. THE MAIN EVENT: You run the bulk of the call (common for SEs and Product)

2. THE STORYTELLER: You use stories to elevate the conversation and engage power

3. THE BAD GUY: You have the hard conversation so the AE can protect goodwill

4. THE ROOM READER: You watch the room while the AE runs the demo

From there: My two favorite tag team plays are:

The Question Elevator:

The VP jumps in to go deeper on something, asks 1-2 questions, then DMs the rep "back to you" so the AE picks it up at a higher altitude.

The Story Baton Pass:

Before launching into a demo riff, you say "Our VP has a good take on this one," so they're ready when you pause.

Running a complex multi-threaded sales cycle?

You gotta master the "recap and roll"

You have a killer disco call with your champion.

Now you get to power.

Most people screw up the executive meeting in one of two extremes:

1. THEY RESTART DISCOVERY entirely (and fatigue the living daylights out of their prospect)

2. THEY SKIP DISCOVERY entirely (and pretend the executive is in full agreement with the champion)

Instead, recap and roll.

RECAP: Everything you've learned in discovery.
ROLL: In new learnings from every new stakeholder.

Just ask: "Here are the 4 things we've learned from your team. What should we add, subtract, or double underline?"

This allows you to run 15 minute compressed discovery calls with every new stakeholder so they can add their perspective.

Without starting the sales cycle from scratch.

I am a recovering "let me do it myself-er"

So here are 5 things I'm trying to be a better delegator, leader, and founder:

1. Shuttle running everything.

It's a common delegation mistake to just chuck something at someone and ask them to come back with a finished product.

Then it comes back so far off from your initial vision that it's unsalvageable (which ruins morale for both you and the employee).

Instead chunk every project into milestones (pick a prospect, come back, write an email, come back, etc) so nothing blows up at the finish line.

2. Acknowledging that mistakes ARE the learning process.

Mistakes are a good thing. They allow you to burn a new neural pathway into someone's mind whenever you correct a behavior.

Instead of getting frustrated when someone makes a mistake, be happy that they made it with you instead of on their biggest deal ever.

3. Do not expect your team to be mindreaders

Oftentimes, a mistake is actually a sign that *I* did not communicate my expectations clearly as a leader (which is learning for me).

Instead of thinking: "Why didn't this person get it right?"

Think: "What context do I know that I didn't share with them?"

4. Say "good job" when something went right.

This sounds obvious but it's easy to focus on what needs to be fixed.

Half of the battle is fixing mistakes.
The other half is doing what you did right AGAIN.

Usually more goes right than wrong.

5. Keep a "you do" list instead of a "to do" list

It was so easy for me to focus on what *I* was getting done in a day.

Instead, right now I'm trying to focus on what I can help my team get done.

Sometimes a great day is me just spending the entire day on blocking my team and supporting them (and not having a PERFECT deep work day myself).

***

You can expect things to go right the first time.

Or you can expect "mistakes" to be part of the coaching process.

One is far more enjoyable than the other.

Sales can still change your life even if you never become an AE.

Many of the most successful sellers I've hired took 1 of 5 paths:

1. Founders: Only 2 things matter when starting a company: building and selling.

(as pictured, Nick and I CERTAINLY would have IPO'd our protein vending machine if we started it AFTER we had sales experience!)

2. RevOps: I know one top AE who climbed to the top of the RevOps org. Everyone in sales loved him because he'd rollout things that would *actually* get adopted (no 17-step deal desk approvals)

3. SDR Leadership: This is where I cut my teeth and honestly I've never had more fun than running the SDR bullpen.

(Also, there's something really, really special when you see an SDR who used to be a nervous kiddo on the phone make it all the way to enterprise, which I'm fortunate to see now.)

4. Account Executive: This is the de-facto path that many take. And if you wanna close, GO CLOSE!!!!

5. Marketers (Especially Content): You can INSTANTLY smell when sales content has been written by a past seller versus a marketer. The best marketers in sales tech WERE sellers (most famously, gong's content team started this way).

Sales teaches you to work hard, get people to open up in conversation, and understand executive business problems at a very young age.

You can take that anywhere.
